# Discipleship Compass

This is the master direction file for the Growing Together Discipleship Series. It is the single source of truth for who we are writing for, how we write, and what makes a lesson good.

All other project files defer to this one on matters of philosophy, tone, audience, and lesson standards.

## Who we are

Neighborhood Church, Overland Park, Kansas. A gospel-centered church plant focused on life-on-life discipleship, Neighborhood Groups, and helping people belong to the family of God, not just attend.

Core phrase: "Loving God and our neighbors together."

Dave Partin is the pastor and primary author. AI assistants serve as editor and development partner.

## Core mission

Build a reproducible discipleship path that helps a person move from new believer toward steady growth, fruitful mission, and eventual leadership.

This project is not trying to create a content library. It is trying to create a discipleship process that people can use for themselves and then use with others.

## Main outcome

The learner should not only be able to say "I understood this." The learner should increasingly be able to say:

- I can live this.
- I can explain this.
- I can take someone else through this.

## Primary learner

Design first for a new believer or spiritually young believer.

For the 100 and 200 level courses:

- Aim for senior-in-high-school conceptual maturity.
- Use much simpler sentence-level readability than that. 60% of Americans read at a 6th grade level or below. Keep vocabulary clear, concrete, and non-academic.
- Write clearly enough that a reader with limited reading confidence can still follow.
- Prefer simple words, short sentences, plain explanation, and concrete examples.
- Explain church terms and theological terms when they are necessary.

Do not write primarily for the strongest readers in the church. Write for the most reachable disciple.

## Secondary learner

A more developed Christian should be able to use these lessons to:

- revisit core truths
- gain practical frameworks and tools
- disciple another person with confidence

The material should be simple enough for a new believer to receive and strong enough for a mature believer to reuse.

## Discipleship philosophy: taste, chew, digest

This project is built on a central design principle drawn from Dave's teaching:

- First pass: taste the truth. A learner goes through a lesson for the first time.
- Second pass: chew the truth. A learner takes someone else through the same material.
- Third pass: digest the truth. A learner teaches it two or three times until it becomes part of life.

Lessons should not only help someone receive truth. They should help someone eventually pass it on. Every piece of content should be designed with all three passes in mind.

## The tree metaphor

The entire curriculum is mapped to the growth of a tree, from roots underground to a full orchard. This gives learners a progressive visual that tracks their journey.

- 101: Roots going down. Starting in faith, the foundations.
- 201: The trunk growing strong. Deep gospel theology and identity in Christ.
- 202: Branches extending outward. Growing in character, holiness, and love.
- 203: Fruit on the branches. Living on mission, sharing the gospel, blessing others.
- 301: One tree becomes an orchard. Leading others, multiplying disciples.

The thesis that ties it all together: "We grow deep so we can grow up so we can grow out."

## Curriculum philosophy

The project should move people through a natural Christian growth arc:

- beginning in Christ
- growing in assurance and identity
- learning basic habits of grace
- growing in holiness and love
- joining the life of the church
- living with gospel intentionality
- helping others follow Jesus

The path should feel progressive but not rushed.

## What new believers most need

Keep returning to the main truths and practices that move a person from young faith toward stable growth:

- who God is
- what the gospel is
- how salvation works
- assurance and identity in Christ
- prayer
- Bible reading and Bible study
- repentance and ongoing growth
- the Holy Spirit and daily dependence
- belonging to the church
- loving family and neighbors
- sharing the gospel naturally
- helping someone else follow Jesus

Do not assume a younger believer will connect these ideas without help. Repeat the central truths in fresh ways across the curriculum.

## Tone and feel

The project should feel warm, pastoral, hopeful, practical, deeply biblical, relational, and clear.

The project should not feel academic, preachy, heavy with jargon, cold, overly polished but spiritually distant, or like a class outline with Christian information.

The strongest lesson voice is direct, personal, Scripture-rich, grace-filled, and concrete. Speak like a caring pastor walking beside someone, never like a professor behind a lectern.

## Self-paced language rule

This study is designed to be worked through at a person's own pace. Avoid "we" language, "together" framing, "meetings" references, or anything that assumes a group setting. Write as if speaking directly to one person doing this on their own. It should still feel relational and warm, but not assume a partner or group is present.

## Writing standard

Prefer: short paragraphs, direct sentences, concrete images, memorable analogies, plain speech, one clear idea at a time, smooth transitions.

Avoid: long chains of abstract thought, too many frameworks in one lesson, insider terms without explanation, pressure without grace, broad application with no next step.

Deep truth is welcome. Dense wording is not required.

## Editorial rules

- No em dashes anywhere in lesson content. Use commas, colons, periods, or " - " (space-hyphen-space) instead.
- No bullet points in lesson prose. Write in paragraphs.
- No Augustine quotes (removed from 201-01, do not re-add).
- Tone: warm, pastoral, gospel-centered, grace-based. Not academic.

## Lesson standard

Every lesson should usually have:

- one big truth
- one clear burden
- one practical next step
- one memorable tool, framework, diagram, exercise, or mini webapp when helpful
- enough clarity that the learner could later use it with someone else

Every lesson should help the learner answer:

- What is true?
- Why does it matter?
- What should I do next?
- How would I explain this to someone else?

## Lesson structure

Every lesson follows the same rhythm:

1. Journey recap (1 paragraph, 2-3 sentences) that tells the learner where they have been in the course and how this lesson connects to what came before. This is the first text block in every lesson except the very first lesson of a course, which has a course welcome instead.
2. Opening that draws the reader into the topic with warmth and accessibility.
3. Teaching sections with short paragraphs and Scripture woven in.
4. Reflection questions numbered using the Series.Lesson.Question format (e.g., 3.2).
5. Closing Reflections, the same three questions every time: "What is one key thing you learned from this lesson?" / "What is one step you are going to take this week?" / "What is a prayer request for this week?"

There are no "homework" or "agenda" sections. The lesson is the homework. People finish a lesson at their own pace and move to the next one naturally. The closing questions create a simple rhythm for one-on-one discipleship conversations.

Lessons are designed to be self-paced (15-20 minutes to read and reflect) but short enough to talk through over lunch with a discipler.

## Reproducibility standard

This curriculum must work in three modes:

- self-paced personal growth
- one-on-one discipleship
- later reuse by the learner as they disciple others

A strong lesson does not stop at private reflection. It gives the learner language, categories, and tools they could eventually reuse in conversation.

## Grace-first rule

Grace should come before pressure. The curriculum should repeatedly make clear:

- God acts first.
- The gospel is received before it is applied.
- Obedience is a response to grace, not a way to earn it.
- Christian growth is dependent, not self-powered.

Do not let lessons drift into behavior management.

## Tool-first expectation

Whenever appropriate, include a simple reusable aid such as: a memory device, a short framework, a diagram, a reflection exercise, a discipleship prompt, a personal inventory, or a mini webapp.

These tools are not extras. They are part of making the curriculum memorable and transferable. A learner should leave thinking, "I could use this with a friend."

## Course emphasis

The 100 and 200 level courses are the most important for accessibility, clarity, and reproducibility.

These courses should be: readable for newer believers, warm enough for devotional use, practical enough for immediate obedience, strong enough for disciple-making reuse.

The 300 level direction can remain more open while the earlier courses are solidified.

## Key editorial decisions (established)

These decisions have been made and should not be revisited without Dave's direction:

- Echo of Love framework (201-01): Receive, Respond, Remain. God's love is the starting point, our love is the echo.
- New Tongue analogy (201-01): Loving God is like getting a new tongue. God gives a new heart, what we once ran from we now run toward.
- Greenhouse metaphor (101): The discipleship course creates conditions for growth; God gives the growth (1 Cor. 3:6).
- Prodigal Son as the primary illustration of the Father's love in 201-01.
- Boss vs. Father illustration in 201-01.
- Zephaniah 3:17 as the climax verse of 201-01.
- Trinity preview at end of 201-01: Father loves you, Son saves you, Spirit fills you.
- John 3:1-4, 16-17 for the 101-04 Bible study method lesson (replacing John 8:2-11, which was too heavy for new believers).
- BLESS rhythms (203-02) consolidated to one paragraph per rhythm with a bold key line each.
- Home page tagline verse: Mark 4:20.
- Title font: Forum (Google Fonts).

## Non-negotiables

- Keep the project deeply biblical.
- Keep the gospel central.
- Keep the writing clear.
- Keep the tone warm and pastoral.
- Keep the learner in view.
- Keep the lesson transferable.
- Do not write mainly to impress mature Christians.
- Do not confuse information with formation.

## What success looks like

A successful lesson or course helps a learner: understand the truth, trust Christ more deeply, take one realistic step of obedience, remember the main idea later, use the idea in a discipleship conversation, and grow in confidence to help someone else follow Jesus.

## Ongoing warning

As the project grows, resist these drifts:

- too much complexity too early
- too many parallel visions for the curriculum
- lessons that feel like notes instead of shepherding
- strong theology with weak usability
- mission language without personal practice
- leadership language before Christian basics are stable
