Explanation of the Diagram Structure and Meaning

This diagram visually explains four key Christian concepts:  **Grace, Mercy, Righteousness, and Justification** . The design intentionally combines **simple geometry, minimal words, and symbolic layout** to help learners understand how these ideas relate to one another and to the cross of Jesus.

The diagram consists of  **four boxes arranged in a square pattern** .

The top row contains two  **square boxes** :

* Grace (top left)
* Mercy (top right)

The bottom row contains two  **tall rectangular boxes** :

* Righteousness (bottom left)
* Justification (bottom right)

The shapes are intentional. The shorter words **Grace** and **Mercy** naturally fit into square boxes, while the longer words **Righteousness** and **Justification** create taller rectangles. This subtle design difference helps visually separate the top and bottom ideas while keeping the layout balanced.

Important: **The four boxes do not touch each other.**

There is a small space between all four boxes. When these spaces line up horizontally and vertically, they create the empty shape of a  **cross in the center of the diagram** . The cross is not drawn directly, but the negative space between the boxes forms it. This visually communicates that  **Jesus and the cross are the center of all four concepts** .

Text Orientation

Each box contains two types of text.

1. **The title word (Grace, Mercy, Righteousness, Justification)** is written  **sideways along the side of the box** . This means the viewer must slightly turn their head to read the title. Writing the titles vertically saves space and keeps the inside of the box uncluttered.
2. The explanation sentence inside each box is written **normally (right-side up)** so that it can be read easily.

This creates a visual hierarchy:

* The **sideways title identifies the concept**
* The **upright sentence explains the meaning**

Connections Between the Boxes

The diagram also visually teaches  **two types of relationships** .

Horizontal Connections

The top row connects  **Grace and Mercy** .

These explain **what we receive or do not receive** from God.

Grace: we  **get something we do not deserve** .
Mercy: we  **do not get something we do deserve** .

The bottom row connects  **Righteousness and Justification** .

These explain  **our new status before God** .

Righteousness: we are  **made right and perfect before God** .
Justification: we are  **declared right because Jesus took the punishment** .

Vertical Connections

The left column connects  **Grace and Righteousness** .

Grace is the  **gift** , and righteousness is the **result** of that gift.
Grace gives us the perfection of Christ, which makes us righteous before God.

The right column connects  **Mercy and Justification** .

Mercy removes the punishment we deserve, and justification explains **why** that punishment can be removed: because Jesus took it in our place.

Central Meaning

The empty cross formed between the four boxes represents  **Jesus and His work on the cross** . All four concepts exist because of what Jesus accomplished through His death and resurrection.

Righteousness

Justification

Together, these four ideas explain  **how Jesus restores people to a right relationship with God** .

# Pastor Dave ideas:

* **The Core Definitions**
  In your sermon on Romans 3:27-4:13, "The Joy of Not Boasting," you gave a brilliant, succinct breakdown of these exact terms that you can lift directly for this lesson. You defined **Mercy** as "Not getting a punishment that you do deserve... Not getting something horrible when you have been horrible". You defined **Grace** as a "Gift that you do not deserve - Something amazing when you have not been amazing". You then explained that to be **Justified** is to receive both of these realities—"to receive Grace and Mercy where God no longer holds our sin against us".

When teaching on  **Righteousness** , you emphasize that it is not something we can manufacture. God's character is the absolute standard of perfect righteousness, and we all fall short. But the beauty of the Gospel is that we are given the righteousness and perfection of Jesus as a gift of grace.

**>> Metaphors and Illustrations Flag**
These are gold for your lesson! You have two incredible, relatable metaphors that you use to teach these concepts:

* **The Courtroom (To explain Justification, Mercy, and Grace):** You tell the church to imagine committing a horrible crime and standing before a judge. You preached, "The Just Judge decides that you do not have to pay the fine and do the time in jail, (mercy) and then he buys you a house and a car because he cares for you so much. (grace)". This perfectly illustrates what it means to be justified by God.
* **The Generous Boss (To explain Grace):** When unpacking grace in Romans 5, you compare it to a boss at work. You describe a scenario where a worker makes mistake after mistake all week, slowing everyone down and costing the company money. But at the end of the week, the manager comes in and gives that worker a generous bonus check completely separate from their normal paycheck. You use this to show that God's grace is unmerited favor; it is not a wage we earn for good behavior.
* **The Wheelbarrow on the Tightrope (To explain Faith):** To explain how we actually *receive* this justification, you frequently point to the classic wheelbarrow on a tightrope illustration. You use this to teach new believers that "Faith is trust that leads to action," showing that we must put our full weight and trust into Jesus' saving work, not just mentally agree with it.

**Theological Distinctives & Repeated Themes**
True to your pastoral heartbeat, you never teach these theological terms just to fill people's heads with knowledge. You use them to hammer home your core distinctive: **Grace over performance.** Because we are justified freely, there is absolutely no room for pride or religious scorekeeping. You constantly remind the church that we do not work to earn salvation; rather, "Another does the work, and we believe. We don’t add to the work. The work is done".

You also emphasize that God's mercy and grace prove that He took the "first step" toward us. You preached from Romans 5:8 that "God did not wait for his creation to get their act together... but He chose to come, while mankind were His enemies and chose to Sacrificially die for our sins". Because Jesus took our punishment (mercy) and gave us His perfection (grace), we can now fight sin from a place of freedom and relationship, rather than fighting sin to *earn* a relationship.

**Flagging a Thin Area**
While you cover the *results* of righteousness beautifully, you might want to bring in a bit of outside theological research on the specific doctrine of "Imputed Righteousness" for these growing believers. You often describe the *concept* perfectly (e.g., wrapping ourselves in the goodness and perfection of Jesus so that God sees Him and not our sin), but officially defining the term "imputation" (that Christ's perfect record is legally credited to our account) will help build a really solid theological vocabulary for your discipleship class.

# John Piper ideas:

* Here is how John Piper defines and connects these four glorious realities. These concepts build perfectly on each other to show a new believer the depths of their salvation.

**1. Grace: Power and Pardon for the Undeserving**
For a new believer, it is crucial to understand that grace is not just a past event, but a present and future power.

* **The Definition of Grace:** Grace is the free blessing of God that flows from His heart to guilty, undeserving sinners. It is the absolute opposite of human initiative or merit. If we earned it, it would not be grace.
* **Grace is Power, Not Just Pardon:** Piper emphasizes that grace is not simply leniency or a "letting off the hook" when we have sinned; "Grace is the enabling gift of God not to sin". It is the divine power that actually helps us press on in obedience.
* **"Future Grace":** We must teach new believers not to just look backward at the grace they received at conversion, but to look forward to "future grace." God is a self-replenishing, all-sufficient fountain. We live moment by moment on the strength of God's future grace arriving to help us in every second of our lives.

**2. Mercy: God’s “Hair-Trigger” Compassion for Our Misery**
While grace is God's goodness directed at our *guilt* and lack of merit, mercy is God's goodness directed at our *misery* and suffering.

* **The Plight of the Sinner:** Since sin always brings misery, every act of grace toward us is also an act of mercy.
* **God’s Eagerness to Show Mercy:** A new believer might picture God as reluctant to forgive. Piper shatters this by explaining that  **God loves to show mercy** . God is not a tight-fisted Scrooge or a schoolyard bully; He is a mountain spring that delights to overflow.
* **The "Hair-Trigger" of Mercy:** Piper uses a beautiful illustration: God's anger must be released by a "stiff safety lock," but His mercy has a "hair trigger". He is "slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love" (Exodus 34:6).

**3. Righteousness: God’s Glory and “The Great Exchange”**
To understand why the cross had to happen, a new believer must understand the biblical definition of righteousness—both God's and ours.

* **God's Righteousness:** Piper defines God’s righteousness as His unswerving commitment to honor and display the infinite worth of His own glory. Because all sin is an assault on God's glory, a truly righteous God cannot just sweep sin under the rug. God's righteousness *demanded* that the insult to His glory be punished.
* **Our Righteousness (The Great Exchange):** Because we have no righteousness of our own to offer God, Christ came to live a perfect, obedient life for us. On the cross, a magnificent transaction took place: God laid our damning record of sin onto Jesus, and He credits Christ's perfect righteousness to us. We receive an "alien righteousness"—a perfection that is not our own, but Christ's.

**4. Justification: The Verdict of “Not Guilty”**
Justification is the legal mechanism by which God applies Christ's righteousness to us. It is the bedrock of Christian security.

**A Courtroom Verdict, Not a Process:** Justification is a legal act in the courtroom of heaven. God does not *make* us morally perfect in that instant (that lifelong process is called sanctification); rather, He *declares* us just and innocent. It is an instantaneous verdict: "Not guilty!".

**Received by Faith Alone:** This verdict is received through faith alone, apart from any of our works. Why faith? Because faith is a "peculiarly receiving grace". Faith is the empty hand that simply receives Christ, ensuring that God gets all the glory. Faith links us to Jesus, and because we are united to Him, God views us as having His righteousness.

**Justifying the  *Ungodly* :** One of the most shocking and liberating truths for a new believer is Romans 4:5—God "justifies the ungodly". We don't clean ourselves up first. We are declared righteous  *while we are still ungodly* .

**The Foundation for Fighting Sin:** Why is this important for practical living? Piper teaches that  **"the only sin that can be successfully killed in the Christian life is forgiven sin"** . If we try to fight our sins to *earn* God's acceptance, we nullify grace. We must fight sin from the rock-solid foundation that we are already 100% accepted, justified, and righteous in God's sight.

# Wesley Huff ideas:

* Wesley Huff has spoken extensively on these exact topics on major platforms like *The Diary of a CEO* and  *100 Huntley Street* , breaking them down in ways that are very accessible for new believers. Here is how he explains Grace, Mercy, Righteousness, and Justification:

**Grace, Mercy, and Justice**
Huff uses a very simple, memorable framework to distinguish between these three concepts:

* **Justice** is "getting what you do deserve".
* **Mercy** is "not getting what you do deserve".
* **Grace** is "getting what you don't deserve".

He ties these together by explaining that Jesus, through His death on the cross, fulfilled the justice required by God's holy law. Because justice was satisfied, **mercy** is enacted for those who put their faith in Jesus—meaning they do not get the punishment they deserve. Furthermore, believers receive  **grace** , which is the completely unearned gift of being adopted as a child of God.

**Righteousness and Being "Good"**
When people ask if being a "good person" gets you into heaven, Huff points out a biblical paradox: "The Bible is very clear. All good people go to heaven. But Jesus said, no one is good but God alone". Because of this, Huff argues that heaven is actually not full of "good" people; rather, it is full of  **"people who understand they are not good enough"** . He notes that human efforts to think, feel, or do the right things will never live up to God's perfect standard of righteousness because we are marred by sin.

**Justification (Received, Not Achieved)**
For new Christians, Huff emphasizes that a relationship with God is not a moralistic checklist. You are not justified by tracking how many times you read your Bible, went to church, or successfully avoided lying and stealing. He beautifully summarizes this by saying that  **"salvation is received, not achieved"** .

He often tells audiences that Christians *are* actually saved by works—but  **"it's just Jesus's [works]"** . Because Jesus is God incarnate, He lived the perfect life that we couldn't and made the sacrifice we couldn't make on our behalf to establish a right relationship with God.

**The Burden-Releasing Difference of the Gospel**
Huff compares other worldviews and religions to a form of "survival of the fittest," where you must perform to a certain standard to earn your way. Christianity flips this entirely:  **"the fittest steps down and sacrifices himself for the survival of the weakest"** .

Other belief systems operate on the idea of "do and you're accepted." The Christian Gospel says,  **"You're accepted now. You can do"** . Huff would encourage your new Christians to understand that while they are not saved *by* their good works, they are saved *for* those works. Living a good life simply becomes a natural outpouring of their new, secure identity in Christ.
