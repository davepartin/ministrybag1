### CRU Follow-Up 5: The Protect and Provide Framework
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in Relationships," 1984)*

The central reframe for a purity lesson is this: **behind every negative command from God are two positive purposes — one to protect you and one to provide for you.** God is not a cosmic killjoy. He gives sexual boundaries out of love and wisdom, not restriction.

This is the essential teaching move for 202.09 — establish it before the "what not to do" content arrives. Without this foundation, a purity lesson lands as moralism. With it, it lands as grace.

**Protect / Provide table (from 1 Thessalonians 4:1-8 and broader biblical context):**

| God's command to wait protects you from... | And provides you with... |
|---------------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Images of past relationships in your mind | Freedom to enjoy your future spouse without detraction |
| Mistrust and suspicion in relationships | A foundation of trust for current and future relationships |
| Confusing the intensity of sex with intimacy | True intimacy: knowing the other person outside the physical |
| Fear and consequences of sexually transmitted diseases | Peace of mind entering a marriage commitment |
| Damaged Christian testimony | Integrity in your witness that ministers to others |
| Broken fellowship with God | The abundant life |

**Key passage:** 1 Thessalonians 4:1-8 — Paul is not rebuking the Thessalonians here; he is complimenting them and encouraging them to "excel still more." The purity command comes from a posture of grace, not accusation.

**Teaching note:** The two key words in 1 Thessalonians 4 are "sanctification" (God's comprehensive will that we develop His character in every area of life) and "defraud" (we defraud someone when we cause them to step outside God's boundaries). Purity is both about your own formation and your care for the other person.

### CRU: David and Psalm 51 — The Key Story for Restoration After Moral Failure
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in Relationships," 1984)*

The story that belongs in the restoration section of this lesson is David. What began with a stare led not only to adultery and an unwanted pregnancy, but also to murder. David really blew it. But consider what happened next — Psalm 51.

David admitted his sin and asked God for forgiveness. He didn't hide, rationalize, or minimize. He named it, owned it, and threw himself on God's mercy: "Have mercy on me, O God, according to your unfailing love."

**The key teaching point for 202.09:** The purity lesson doesn't end with the person who has already failed. One of the greatest mistakes people make is assuming that because they have failed to keep God's command regarding sex, God wants nothing to do with them. That is not true. God is well aware of our sinful tendencies — that is why Jesus came.

**Four restoration steps from this passage and surrounding texts:**
1. **Confess the sin** — specifically name it before God (1 John 1:9). Don't generalize; be honest.
2. **Forgive yourself** — refusing to forgive yourself is claiming to be a higher judge than God. Christ's death was sufficient. If God's grace can't cover your sin, it can't cover any sin. Romans 8:1.
3. **Be filled with the Holy Spirit** — allow God's Spirit to fill you with power to say no to future temptation (Ephesians 5:18). Restoration is not just absolution; it's re-equipping.
4. **Show fruits of repentance** — repentance means turning around (Matthew 3:8). This might mean ending a relationship, changing a pattern, or refusing to be alone with someone. Actions should show that something has actually changed.

**Teaching note on self-forgiveness:** This is often the sticking point. People accept God's forgiveness intellectually but keep punishing themselves emotionally. The pastoral word here is clear: when you refuse to forgive yourself, you are functionally saying Christ's death wasn't enough. That's not humility — that's a subtle form of pride that places your guilt above the cross.

### CRU: Daniel 1:8 — The Decision Point
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in Relationships," 1984)*

"But Daniel made up his mind that he would not defile himself with the king's choice food or with the wine which he drank."

**The application for 202.09:** The purity decision is made in advance, not in the moment. Daniel didn't decide when the food arrived in front of him — he had already decided. When the temptation came, the decision was already settled. He didn't need to debate it in the moment because the question had already been answered.

**Key word — defile:** To become impure. Daniel's decision was not primarily about food preferences; it was about refusing to be shaped by the culture around him when that culture conflicted with his covenant identity.

**Teaching note:** This is a powerful closing story for the lesson's application turn. The question for each person is not just "are you committed to purity?" but "when did you decide?" The Daniel story frames purity as a pre-made commitment, not a reaction to circumstances. It also connects naturally to the accountability and fellowship application steps — because Daniel didn't make that decision alone; he made it with his friends.

**Result:** Daniel 1:9-13 — God gave Daniel favor with the commander, and the test bore out that his choice was honored. Purity is not just obedience; it is wisdom that produces unexpected blessing.

### Supporting Practices for Maintaining Purity
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in Relationships," 1984)*

Four practical supports for a life of purity — useful as the lesson's application structure:

1. **Prayer and Bible Study** — Regularly spending time with God shapes what you want and how you think (Psalm 1:1-3; Ephesians 5:8-10; Jeremiah 9:23-24).
2. **Spirit-Filled Life** — The Holy Spirit empowers the "no" that willpower alone cannot sustain (Galatians 5:16).
3. **Accountability** — Our accountability to another person reinforces our accountability to God (Ecclesiastes 4:9-10; Proverbs 27:17; Ephesians 5:21).
4. **Fellowship** — Seek community with people who strengthen your convictions, not ones who erode them (Hebrews 10:24-25; 2 Timothy 2:22).

**Teaching note:** These four are not the lesson's content — they're the landing pad. After the theology and the stories, the lesson needs to leave people with something they can actually do tomorrow. These four practices give it traction.
