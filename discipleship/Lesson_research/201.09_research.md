# 201.09: Becoming Who You Already Are: Fruit of the Spirit

## Overview

**Key Verse:** Galatians 5:22-23
**Description:** Sanctification is the process, the Fruit of the Spirit is the evidence. God progressively conforms you to Christ's image through the Spirit.
**Goal:** Understand sanctification as God's lifelong work and recognize the fruit of the Spirit as evidence of transformation.

## Baptist Faith & Message 2000

- Article IV: Salvation (sanctification) — Sanctification is the process by which the Holy Spirit progressively transforms believers to be more like Christ.

True love isn't dutiful forced actions; it's joyful caring, compassion, and actions rooted in God's grace.

**Dave Sermon Galatians 5:22 #3 Fruit of the Spirit pt 3 - faithfulness, gentleness, self-control

Good morning, Neighborhood Church! We're continuing our three-week journey through the fruit of the Spirit from Galatians 5:22-23, anchored in our summer theme: "Christ Living in Us." Last week, Joel walked us through patience, kindness, and goodness, those beautiful expressions of what God pours out of us when we're filled with His Spirit. Today, we're completing our exploration with the final three: faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control.

>> Illustration: But before we dive in, let me tell you about my childhood in Lisbon Falls, Maine. When I was 8 we moved from Florida, was absolutely thrilled because right in our yard stood what I thought was a fruit tree. Now, if you've ever been to Maine, you know apple trees are everywhere, lining front yards, backyards, and country roads. Every fall, you see branches sagging under the weight of red, green, or golden apples. And I thought, This is it! We have our own apple tree!
>>

That first year, I waited patiently for the apples to grow. In the spring, the blossoms were beautiful. The bark, the leaves, it all looked just like the other apple trees in the neighborhood. But then the fruit started to grow… and it was not what I was expecting. Tiny, hard, sour crabapples.

Here's the thing, apple trees and crabapple trees can look almost identical in the spring and summer. Same bark. Same leaves. Same general shape. To the untrained eye, you might think they're the same. But once the fruit comes, you know exactly what you're dealing with. The fruit reveals the truth.

Jesus said in Matthew 7:16, "You will recognize them by their fruits." The same principle applies to us as Christians. People can't always tell from the "bark" and "leaves" of our lives what's really inside. But when the fruit starts to show, the way we live, the way we love, the way we respond to pressure, that's when they can tell.

Today, we're going to talk about three pieces of fruit the Holy Spirit grows in us: faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. And just like with apple and crabapple trees, the world will know who we truly belong to by the fruit we bear.

Let's pray as we open God's Word.

Intro: As we've been studying throughout this summer, Paul wrote Galatians to churches he had planted during his first missionary journey. These were communities of both Jewish and Gentile believers who had initially received the Gospel with great joy. However, false teachers had infiltrated these young churches, bringing confusion by insisting that believers needed to follow Jewish law, particularly circumcision, to truly belong to Christ.

This wasn't just a theological debate; it was an attack on the very heart of the Gospel. Paul recognized that adding anything to faith in Christ alone wasn't just a minor issue, it was turning to something entirely different, abandoning grace for human performance.

Throughout Galatians 5, Paul has been contrasting two ways of living: walking by the Spirit versus gratifying the desires of the flesh. He's shown us the works of the flesh, those obvious, destructive patterns that lead to spiritual death. But now, in verses 22-23, he paints a beautiful picture of what life looks like when Christ truly lives in us.

These aren't achievements we manufacture through willpower or religious discipline. They're the natural outflow of a life rooted in Jesus, empowered by His Spirit. And the final three, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control, reveal how deeply this transformation goes.

## Point #1 – Faithfulness: Standing Firm When It Costs

Let's read together: Galatians 5:22-23: "But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness..."

Faithfulness means steadfast loyalty, keeping your word, honoring your commitments, and standing firm in what you know is right, even when it's costly or inconvenient.

>> Illustration: An extreme case is seen in one of the films about Dietrich Bonhoeffer, the German pastor imprisoned for resisting Hitler; there’s a striking scene near the end of his life. A guard notices Bonhoeffer’s hands trembling right before his execution and asks, “Are you scared?” Bonhoeffer looks him in the eye and calmly replies, “No… I’m just a little cold.” It’s such a small exchange, but it speaks volumes. Here was a man facing certain death, yet his courage and steadiness flowed from a deeper place ,  his unwavering faith in Christ. Faithfulness doesn’t mean you never feel the chill of fear; it means you stand firm in the calling and truth of God, even when it costs you everything. Bonhoeffer wrote the book “The Cost of Discipleship”
>>

Standing firm when you know what is right is about being dependable in our walk with God and toward others. It's a mark of someone whose roots go deep into Christ.

The Bible repeatedly shows us that faithfulness is God's own character.

Lamentations 3:22–23 says, "The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness."

This is who God is. He has been the one faithful to humanity since creation. And when the Spirit works in us, He reproduces that same steadfastness in our relationships, our work, and our obedience.

Think about Daniel. Carried off to Babylon as a young man, he was pressured to eat the king's food, bow to idols, and stop praying to the true God. Yet in every situation, Daniel remained faithful. His loyalty didn't depend on his comfort; it was rooted in God's truth. When the king's advisors set a trap by convincing Darius to outlaw prayer, Daniel didn't hide. He opened his windows toward Jerusalem and prayed three times a day, just as he always had. That kind of faithfulness is what Paul is calling us to.

But here's the ultimate picture of faithfulness: Picture Jesus hanging on the cross. The crowd mocks Him. The disciples have scattered. He could have called down legions of angels, but instead He stays, enduring the nails, the thirst, and the shame, because He is faithful to His Father's will and to the mission of saving us. That is the ultimate picture of faithfulness.  Challenge: Where in your life is God calling you to keep showing up, even when it's hard or unnoticed? Maybe it's in your marriage when the romance has faded. Maybe it's in parenting when your kids seem ungrateful. Maybe it's in your job when you're overlooked for promotion. Faithfulness says, "I'm here because Christ is faithful to me."

## Point #2 – Gentleness: Strength Under Control

Galatians 5:22-23: "But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness..."

Gentleness often gets misunderstood as weakness, but that couldn't be further from the truth. In Scripture, gentleness is strength under control, like a soldier holding a rescued child. That soldier has the strength to destroy, but in this moment, his strength is used to protect, comfort, and serve.

Proverbs 16:24 "Gracious words are like a honeycomb, sweetness to the soul and health to the body."

Proverbs 15:1 "A soft answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger."

Proverbs 25:15 "With patience a ruler may be persuaded, and a soft tongue will break a bone."

>> Illustration: Strength Under Control. The image of a stong soldier in full gear who has a child he just rescued.  Meekness - Strength Under Control
>>

Picture Jesus at the table with sinners and tax collectors (The very power to Judge all the sin in the world). Picture Him kneeling down to wash His disciples' feet, even Judas'. Picture Him reaching out to touch the leper when everyone else recoiled in fear. That is the gentleness of the King of Kings, strength used not to crush, but to restore.

A crabapple tree's fruit is harsh, biting, and sour. You bite into it and instantly pucker. But a ripe apple is sweet and nourishing. Gentleness is that sweetness of spirit that draws people in instead of pushing them away. healthy to the body, turns away wrath, and the power it has.

Challenge: How do your words and actions make others feel, safe or defensive? Who needs you to respond with gentleness this week? Maybe it's your spouse when they interrupt your plans. Maybe it's your coworker who's struggling with a project. Maybe it's the cashier who's having a bad day. Gentleness says, "I'm going to use my strength to lift you up, not tear you down."

Point #3 – Self-Control: Discipline for True Freedom

Galatians 5:22-23: "But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control; against such things there is no law.”

Self-control is the Spirit-given ability to govern your desires and impulses so that they serve God’s purposes instead of ruling over you. Proverbs 25:28 says, “A man without self-control is like a city broken into and left without walls.”Without it, we are vulnerable to every temptation, distraction, and destructive pattern.

When people hear the word discipline, they often think of restriction, rules that take away enjoyment. But biblical self-control is not about restriction for its own sake. It’s about freedom, freedom to walk closely with God, freedom from the grip of sin, freedom to love others well without being mastered by your own appetites. Paul says in 1 Corinthians 9:27, “I discipline my body and keep it under control, lest after preaching to others I myself should be disqualified.” He knew that without discipline, even the most gifted servant of God could lose the very race they were running.

>> Illustration: My neighbor back in Maine had a row of beautiful apple trees. Every spring, I’d see him out there with his sprayer, treating them for bugs. Later in the season, he’d wrap protective barriers around the base of the trunks to keep pests and critters from climbing up. I remember asking him once, “Isn’t that a lot of work?” He just smiled and said, “It’s the only way to get apples worth eating. If you don’t protect them, you’re just growing food for the worms.” That’s exactly what self-control is like, guarding against what would spoil what God is growing in your life. It’s not restriction for the sake of restriction; it’s discipline for the sake of true freedom, freedom to enjoy the good fruit without it being ruined by the enemy’s influence.
>>

Picture Jesus in the wilderness, fasting for 40 days. Satan comes with every temptation, turn these stones into bread, throw yourself from the temple, bow down and I’ll give you the kingdoms of the world. Yet Jesus answers with Scripture every time. Hungry, tired, and physically weak, He remains completely in control, choosing His Father’s will over His immediate desires. That’s discipline for true freedom: the power to obey God when everything in you wants the easier way.

Do It - This kind of self-control doesn’t happen by accident. It’s built over time, through daily habits that shape your mind and heart. It might mean setting boundaries around what you watch, how you spend your money, or the way you respond when provoked. It might mean training your body and soul to pause, pray, and listen before speaking. Every act of Spirit-led discipline is a step toward the freedom God intends for His children, a life unhindered by the chains of sin and distraction.

* Challenge: Where do you need discipline for true freedom in your life right now?
* Is it in your thoughts, training your mind to reject lies and hold to truth?
* In your habits, choosing what builds you up rather than what drains you?
* In your relationships, responding with grace instead of reacting in anger?

Self-control is not about proving yourself to God; it’s about walking in the freedom He’s already given you through Christ. The Spirit equips you for this, so that nothing can master you except the One who made you.

## The Christ Connection

Here's what makes all of this possible: these qualities aren't just moral improvements we're trying to achieve. They're the very character of Christ Himself. Jesus was perfectly faithful, enduring the cross for the joy set before Him. He was perfectly gentle, welcoming children and healing the broken. He had perfect self-control, resisting every temptation and submitting completely to His Father's will.

The Gospel tells us we were enemies of God, slaves to our flesh, producing the bitter fruit of sin. We deserved God's wrath, not His favor. But Jesus died for our sins, rose victorious over death, and sends His Spirit to live in us. When we repent of our sin and believe in Him, He doesn't just forgive us, He transforms us from the inside out.

This fruit of the Spirit isn't something we manufacture through sheer willpower. It's the natural result of Christ living in us, of being connected to the true vine. John 15:5 reminds us, "I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever abides in me and I in him, he it is that bears much fruit.”

Conclusion: Known by Our Fruit

In the same way, the world will know who we are, not by what church we attend or what label we claim, but by the fruit they see in our lives. Faithfulness that endures under pressure. Gentleness that uses strength to restore, not crush. Self-control that protects what God is doing in us.The fruit of the spirit does not save you. It is faith in Jesus that does that. The fruit of the Spirit reveals to ourselves and the world that we are saved.

Fruit of the Spirit John Piper


**The Fruit of the Spirit is the organic, inevitable result of being united to Jesus Christ by faith.** The Christian life is not about trying to manufacture moral behaviors through sheer willpower; rather, it is about staying vitally connected to the life-giving vine so that the sap of the Holy Spirit produces the fruit of the kingdom.

Here are John Piper’s top three focuses regarding what it means to live out the fruit of the Spirit and why it is universally important:

**1. Fruit-Bearing Replaces Law-Keeping**
The fruit of the Spirit is the result of the new covenant. Formerly, righteousness was demanded from the outside by letters written in stone, which only condemned and killed. Now, because of the cross, we belong to the living Christ, and His Spirit writes the law on our hearts.  **We have died to the law through the body of Christ so that we might belong to Him and "bear fruit for God" (Romans 7:4)** . The fruit of the Spirit (love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control) proves that  **sin no longer has dominion over us because we are not under law but under the liberating mercy of God’s grace** .

**2. Faith in Future Grace is the Root of the Fruit**
While the Holy Spirit is the decisive, miracle-working cause of the fruit in our lives, He always empowers this fruit through the channel of our faith. Paul asks, "Does he who supplies the Spirit to you... do so by works of the law, or by hearing with faith?" (Galatians 3:5).  **The Holy Spirit loves to glorify Jesus, so He keeps Himself quietly beneath the surface and makes our faith in Christ's promises the conscious cause of our fruit** . We bear the fruit of the Spirit by trusting that all God promises to be for us in Jesus is infinitely more satisfying than the deceitful promises of sin.

**3. The Ultimate Importance of Fruit is the Display of God's Glory**
God requires the fruit of the Spirit in His people to make their invisible faith visible as a public display of the beauty and worth of Christ.  **"God is shown to be glorious by the good deeds of Christians"** . When we are freed from fear, pride, and greed, we are filled with a zeal to love others the way we have been loved. Because this fruit-bearing behavior is contrary to human self-enhancement and self-preservation, attention is drawn directly to God as our life-transforming Treasure.

### Deep Dive into Patience, Kindness, and Goodness

When studying the specific fruits of Patience, Kindness, and Goodness, Piper offers profound, heart-exposing insights for growing believers:

#### Patience: Trusting God's Unplanned Pace

Impatience is fundamentally a form of unbelief. It springs up in our hearts when our plans are interrupted or shattered, causing us to doubt the wisdom of God’s timing or the goodness of His guidance.

* **Waiting in the Unplanned Place:** Piper defines patience as  **"the capacity to 'wait and to endure' without murmuring and disillusionment—to wait in the unplanned place, and endure the unplanned pace"** .
* **A Display of Inner Strength:** Impatient people are actually weak, depending on external supports like perfectly orchestrated schedules to keep their fragile hearts intact. True patience demands tremendous inner strength, which Paul says comes "according to his glorious might" (Colossians 1:11).
* **The Key is Future Grace:** We battle impatience by trusting that God is sovereignly up to something good for us in all our delays and detours. Joseph, for instance, spent years in slavery and prison—a massive test of patience—but was sustained by the faith that God would turn the unplanned pace into a magnificent outcome ("God meant it for good," Gen 50:20).

#### Kindness: The Overflow of God's Mercy

Kindness is not just an outward duty but an inward affection—an experience of "tenderheartedness" (Ephesians 4:32).

* **Imitating the Father:** Jesus commands us to "love your enemies, and do good... and you will be sons of the Most High, for he is kind to the ungrateful and the evil" (Luke 6:35).  **We show kindness to prove that we have been transformed into the likeness of a God who is kind to those who do not deserve it** .
* **Delighting in Mercy:** God does not just want us to do kind things; He commands us to "love kindness" (Micah 6:8). Genuine kindness is an overflow of joy in God that *gladly* meets the needs of others. If we do not find pleasure in our acts of kindness, we fail to reflect the heart of God, who delights in showing mercy.

#### Goodness: A Passion for Good Works

The fruit of "goodness" is heavily tied to the biblical concept of doing "good works."

* **The Fruit, Not the Root:** Good deeds are never the foundation of our acceptance with God; they are the necessary fruit of it. God saved us "not because of our works" (2 Timothy 1:9), but rather, we are "created in Christ Jesus for good works" (Ephesians 2:10).
* **Zeal for the Vulnerable:** Christ died specifically "to purify for himself a people... who are  **zealous for good works** " (Titus 2:14). This means Jesus did not die just to make goodness possible, but to produce a passionate *zeal* for it. Without limiting their scope, Piper notes that the Bible emphasizes good works as  **"mainly helping people in urgent need, especially those who possess least and suffer most"** .
* **The Best Life:** Stepping into the messiness of other people's lives to do good is the ultimate Christian calling. No matter what it costs us in this world, goodness is the best life because:  **"They get help, we get joy, God gets glory"** .




Fruit of the Spirit Commentary


 **Fruit of the Spirit** . Expression taken from Galatians 5:22, 23. As listed there, this fruit is the manifest evidence one may expect from a life in which the Spirit of God is living and reigning. Jesus also implied that the character of a life can be determined. In the context of Matthew 7, the test for false prophets is, among other things, the kind of life they live. Consequently, even though Jesus did prohibit censorious criticism of others by his followers (Mt 7:1), he encouraged fruit inspection. The secret to exhibiting spiritual fruit in abundance is described in John 12:24. Using as an illustration a grain of wheat sown in the ground, Jesus encourages death to self and to the desires of the old nature and resurrection to the new life of “much fruit.”

The fruit of the Spirit, as listed in Galatians 5, is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. Love is that outgoing, self-giving kind of action, not necessarily emotion, that characterized God himself when he loved the world so much that he *gave* his only Son (Jn 3:16). Goodness is the translation of a Greek word that includes the idea of generosity. The word “faith” refers usually to trust or confidence in someone or something. However, the word can also refer to that which causes trust and faith, namely faithfulness and reliability. Both meanings are in the use of the word here as another evidence of the Spirit-controlled life. Another fruit, translated “temperance” by the kjv, is the Greek word for self-control, that ability to hold oneself in, to keep oneself in check. It is significant that the Spirit is said to be the one responsible for this fruit. Since these qualities are the fruit of the Spirit, it is self-evident that legalism and obedience to law cannot originate or produce them.

This fruit appears in a context within Galatians where Paul is emphasizing Christian freedom from obedience to the Law as a meritorious means of being justified before God. He warns the Galatian Christians, who were in danger of returning to the Law, that physical circumcision is an outward sign of a return to legalistic means of justification and that to attempt to obtain it this way is impossible (Gal 5:3). However, lest the Galatians overemphasize their freedom in Christ, Paul cautions that this liberty does not mean license to sin, an opportunity for gratifying the desires of the flesh, but rather an opportunity to continue as loving bondservants belonging to one another (v 13). Life in the Spirit will mean that one will not fulfill the lusts or desires of the flesh (v 16). Then Paul identifies both the works of the flesh and the fruit of the Spirit. A person who is abstaining from the works of the flesh and is demonstrating the fruit of the Spirit in his life will be loving, joyful, peaceful, patient, kind, good, dependable, gentle, and self-controlled. These are not said to be gifts of the Spirit, however, but graces that will adorn the life that is under the Holy Spirit’s control.

There has been some confusion about the relation of this fruit to the gifts (Greek,  *charismata* ) of the Spirit listed in 1 Corinthians 12:4–11; 12:28–31a; Romans 12:3–8; and Ephesians 4:11–14. First Corinthians 12:31b explicitly relates the gifts of the Spirit to the fruit. The first fruit of the Spirit mentioned in Galatians 5:22 is love, the very item which Paul mentions as “a more excellent way” (1 Co 12:31b) rather than as a gift to be listed with the others.

It is significant that the works of the flesh, are many, while the fruit of the Spirit is singularly one. Hence some have interpreted Galatians 5:22 to mean that the fruit of the Spirit is singular, namely, love, and that what follows (vv 22, 23) are varying facets of love. Correlating this Galatians reference with 1 Corinthians 12:31b demonstrates that the fruit of the Spirit is love with all of its many aspects and also the more excellent *way* in which the various gifts are to be used. To underscore that love is not itself a gift, Paul goes on to describe the fruit of the Spirit, in 1 Corinthians 13, the great love chapter of the Bible. Comparing these two passages it is interesting to note how many of the facets of love appear in both places. Paul is saying in essence that the gifts of the Spirit are only effective, worthwhile, and capable of edifying, when they are exercised in a proper  *way* , that is, with the qualities given in Galatians 5:22 and 23 and 1 Corinthians 13.

It becomes apparent then that the fruit of the Spirit and not the gifts or any particular gift is the evidence of a Spirit-controlled life. The Corinthians were blessed with many of the gifts (1 Cor 1:5, 7; chs 12; 14), but they were still immature (1 Cor 3:1–4). The proof of a Spirit-led, sanctified life is not the gifts of the Spirit but the fruit of the Spirit, chief of which is love.
