# 201.03: The Spirit-Filled Walk: God the Holy Spirit

## Overview

**Key Verse:** Galatians 5:25
**Description:** The Father loves you. The Son redeems you. The Spirit lives in you. Completes the Trinity sequence.
**Goal:** Understand who the Holy Spirit is and learn to walk in His power daily.

## Notes



The foundation of walking in the Spirit lies in understanding that the Christian life is impossible to live by human strength alone. The most prominent teaching centers on the distinction between merely having the Spirit indwell you at salvation and being continuously filled, directed, and empowered by Him. Believers are called to claim this filling by faith, relying on two key realities: God's command in Ephesians 5:18 to "be filled with the Spirit," By surrendering control to Christ and asking in faith, growing Christians can transition from a frustrated, self-directed life to a Christ-directed life marked by supernatural power and fruitfulness.

a foundational concept is that the gospel is not just the entry point into the kingdom of God; it is the entire dynamic for our ongoing spiritual growth. A common trap for growing Christians is falling into moralism, operating on the religious principle of "I obey, therefore I am accepted". However, the Spirit works through the gospel principle: "I am accepted through Christ, therefore I obey". A lack of deep belief in this gospel grace is the root cause of spiritual deadness, fear, and pride. When believers apply the gospel deeply to their hearts, the Holy Spirit restructures their motivations and self-identity, changing them from the inside out rather than just modifying their external behavior.

As believers walk in the Spirit, the primary evidence is the transformation of their character through the fruit of the Spirit, as outlined in Galatians 5:22-23: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. It is vital to understand that the word "fruit" is singular, meaning these traits operate as a unified whole, much like the many facets of a single diamond. A person cannot truly possess one trait, such as self-control, without also possessing love and joy; otherwise, it is merely a counterfeit produced by human willpower and pride. The Spirit’s ultimate goal is to cultivate this Christlike character, which is far more important to God than any service or ministry we might attempt to render without it.

Growing Christians must be taught to expect an ongoing, internal battle between their old sinful nature (the flesh) and their new divine nature (the Spirit). Galatians 5:17 warns that these two forces are constantly fighting against each other to win control over our lives. Understanding the pervasive power of indwelling sin helps believers avoid shock or despair when they stumble, realizing that this is a normal part of the Christian experience on this side of eternity. Instead of relying on their own willpower to fight this war, believers must actively call upon the Holy Spirit to help them put to death the misdeeds of the body (Romans 8:13), yielding to His control when temptation strikes.

A critical, though sometimes overlooked, aspect of walking in the Spirit is learning not to depend on fluctuating emotions. The relationship between truth and emotion is best illustrated by the analogy of a train: "Fact" (God and His Word) is the engine, "Faith" (our trust in God) is the fuel car, and "Feeling" is the caboose. The train runs smoothly on the power of the engine and the fuel, regardless of whether the caboose is present. It is futile to try to pull a train by its caboose, just as it is futile for Christians to base their spiritual walk on seeking emotional experiences. Valid emotions are simply the byproduct of faith and obedience, so believers must anchor their walk in the unchanging promises of God's Word rather than their current emotional state.

A slightly more nuanced, yet urgently needed, teaching for today's hyper-connected world is the necessity of unplugging to cultivate intimacy with the Holy Spirit. Even Jesus, despite the massive demands on His time, intentionally withdrew from the crowds to experience quiet communion with the Father (Mark 1:35). The modern lack of intimacy with God is often due to our refusal to shut off communication from others so we can be alone with Him. By practicing the spiritual discipline of stillness and cutting out the surrounding din of the world, believers can sensitize their hearts to recognize the Holy Spirit's promptings and experience deep fellowship with Him.

Finally, many growing Christians become paralyzed by anxiety over discovering "God's long-term will" for their lives, such as career choices or geographic moves. However, a truly Spirit-led life often requires us to forget about our obsession with the future and focus entirely on submitting to the Spirit's leading in the present moment. God is profoundly more concerned with our obedience to His Spirit today than with our plans for next year. The Spirit's clearly revealed will is for our daily sanctification—conforming us to the image of Christ, putting to death the deeds of the flesh, and acting in holiness in our immediate circumstances. As we walk faithfully in the Spirit day by day, He organically directs the larger trajectory of our lives.

### CRU Follow-Up 3: Three Kinds of People — Natural / Carnal / Spiritual

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

CRU's classic diagram distinguishes three kinds of people based on 1 Corinthians 2:14–3:2:

**Natural** (1 Cor. 2:14) — Has not trusted Christ. The Holy Spirit does not indwell them.

**Carnal** (1 Cor. 3:1-2) — Has trusted Christ, but is living in their own strength and effort. Self is still on the throne. The Spirit indwells but is not directing the life. Living as a Christian in the energy of the flesh.

**Spiritual** (1 Cor. 2:15) — Has trusted Christ and is trusting in the Holy Spirit's power. Christ has been placed in control. Self is yielding to Christ's direction.

**The key teaching point:** All Christians are *indwelt* by the Spirit (Romans 8:9) — but not all Christians are *filled* with (directed and empowered by) the Spirit. The Spirit's presence is settled at salvation. The Spirit's control is a daily, moment-by-moment reality that requires surrender.

**Teaching note:** This framework helps people name where they actually are rather than pretending. The carnal Christian category is often where people get stuck — they know they're saved but they're exhausted from trying to live the Christian life by willpower. The lesson's goal is to show them there's a third option they're missing.

### CRU: The Computer Analogy for the Spirit-Filled Life

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

"This computer has all the potential to do what it was created to do — but it has no power of its own."

Just as a computer needs to be plugged in to do what it was designed to do, a Christian has all the potential in their new identity in Christ — but that potential only becomes power when they draw from the right source. You were designed to run on the Holy Spirit. Trying to live the Christian life on self-effort is like running a computer on the wrong power source.

**Teaching note:** Simple, accessible illustration for younger or newer Christians who have been grinding hard at the Christian life and burning out.

### CRU: The Four Purposes of the Holy Spirit

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

Why did the Holy Spirit come?

1. To glorify Christ (John 16:14)
2. To lead you into all truth (John 16:13)
3. To dwell in you (Romans 8:9)
4. To convict the world of sin and justice (John 16:8)

He also produces fruit (Galatians 5:22-23), bears witness of our sonship (Romans 8:14-16), and equips for witness (Acts 1:8).

**Teaching note:** Purpose #1 is worth lingering on — the Spirit's primary ambition is not your comfort or your empowerment, it's Christ's glory. A Spirit-filled believer becomes less focused on self and more focused on Jesus. That's the diagnostic.

### CRU: Desire / Confess / Yield — Preparing to Be Filled

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

Three ways to prepare your heart to be filled with the Spirit:

1. **Desire** sincerely to be filled (Matthew 5:6 — "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness"). The filling begins with a genuine want, not a ritual.
2. **Confess** all known sin (1 John 1:9; Psalm 66:18). Unconfessed sin quenches the Spirit's direction.
3. **Yield** your total life to Christ (Romans 12:1-2). Yielding means willingness to obey in anything God has shown you to do.

Then respond by faith, trusting the Holy Spirit to empower your life (Ephesians 5:18; 1 John 5:14-15).

### CRU: Spiritual Breathing — The Ongoing Practice

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

The "Spiritual Breathing" metaphor is the most practical teaching tool in this entire series for the ongoing Spirit-filled life. It gives people a repeatable, daily mechanism rather than treating the filling as a one-time event.

**Exhale — Confess your sin** (1 John 1:9; Hebrews 10:1-25). As soon as you become aware of sin — an attitude, an action, a word — breathe it out. Agree with God that it is sin. Don't wait until the end of the day; deal with it in the moment.

**Inhale — Yield control of your life to Christ** (Romans 12:1-2). Claim by faith the fullness of the Holy Spirit (Ephesians 5:18). Trust that He now directs and empowers you (1 John 5:14-15).

**Teaching note:** The metaphor works because breathing is involuntary and constant — it's not something you do once and forget. The spiritual parallel is that walking in the Spirit is not a one-time crisis experience but a continuous rhythm of surrender and trust. The more natural this rhythm becomes, the more the character of Christ gets produced in you over time.

**Connection to key verse (Galatians 5:25):** "Since we live by the Spirit, let us keep in step with the Spirit." Spiritual Breathing is a concrete picture of what "keeping in step" looks like in daily life.

### Maturity Takes Time — No Shortcuts

*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in the Spirit," 1984)*

"The degree to which these traits are manifested in the life depends upon the extent to which the Christian trusts the Lord with every detail of his life, and upon his maturity in Christ. One who is only beginning to understand the ministry of the Holy Spirit should not be discouraged if he is not as fruitful as more mature Christians who have known and experienced this truth for a longer period."

**Teaching note:** This is a grace note worth including in the lesson — especially for younger or newer believers who feel like they should be "getting it" faster. The Spirit-filled life is a direction, not an arrival. The graph goes up over time, not in a straight line.
