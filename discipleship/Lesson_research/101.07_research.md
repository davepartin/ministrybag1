# 101.07: A Life of Repentance

## Dave's Ideas — Add These
*(Raw ideas to develop — place toward the top of the lesson)*

**God Has Always Cared About Purity in the Community**
In the Old Testament, God was not casual about sin in the midst of His people. Purity was not just a personal matter — it was communal, covenantal, and worshipful. When sin was allowed to fester, it hurt the individual, it hurt the community, and it corrupted the worship of God. God cared about cutting out what hurts us, what hurts our community, and what hurts our worship. That thread runs straight through the Bible.

**Ananias and Sapphira (Acts 5)**
The New Testament doesn't soften this. Ananias and Sapphira lied about their gift to the church — they gave money but secretly held some back while pretending to give it all. God struck them both dead. This is one of the most jarring passages in the New Testament for a reason: it shows that God takes the purity of the church community seriously. It's not just about personal sin. It's about what dishonesty and performance do to the body of Christ. Use this carefully — not to terrify new believers, but to establish that repentance is not optional housekeeping, it's the ongoing health of the community.

**My Heart's Christ Home — The Closet**
*(Robert Boyd Munger, booklet published 1954)*
In Munger's classic booklet, the narrator invites Jesus into their heart as a home. Jesus walks through every room — the library, the dining room, the living room — and the narrator is happy to show Him around. But there is one closet. The narrator keeps it locked. The smell leaks out from under the door. Finally Jesus says He wants that closet cleaned out too. The narrator eventually hands over the key, and Jesus cleans it completely.

The application for repentance: most people are happy for Jesus to be in the living room — the visible, public parts of their life. Repentance is handing Him the key to the closet that nobody else knows about. Use this near the end of the lesson as the application illustration — the thing nobody wants to do but everyone knows they need to do. The promise is that when the closet is cleaned out, we can run free.

## Overview
**Key Verse:** 1 John 1:9
**Description:** Frames repentance as an ongoing rhythm of the heart — turning from sin and back to God — that keeps your faith alive and your walk with Jesus moving forward.

### CRU Follow-Up 2: Relationship vs. Fellowship Distinction
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in God's Love," 1984)*

One of the most useful teaching tools for this lesson is the distinction between **relationship** and **fellowship** with God — because it answers the question new believers always have: "Does sin make me lose my salvation?"

**Relationship** with God:
- Began when you received Christ (John 1:12)
- Is everlasting (1 Peter 1:3-4)
- Maintained by God (John 10:27-29)
- Never changes (Hebrews 13:5)

**Fellowship** with God:
- Began when you received Christ (Colossians 2:6)
- Can be hindered (Psalm 32:3-5)
- Maintained in part by us (1 John 1:9)
- Changes when we sin (Psalm 66:18)

**The father-child illustration:** Your relationship with your earthly father is permanent. No matter what you do, you will always be his child. But suppose you rebelled against your father and angrily left home in spite of his efforts to reconcile. You would still be his child — but what would happen to your fellowship? This is the distinction that keeps repentance from being about fear of losing salvation and roots it instead in the desire for restored intimacy. We confess not to re-earn sonship but to come home.

**Teaching note:** This distinction is the pastoral heart of this lesson. Without it, people either minimize sin (because they know they're secure) or live in fear (wondering if they're still saved). The relationship/fellowship framework holds both truths at once.

### CRU: The "Paid in Full" Illustration — Colossians 2:13-14
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in God's Love," 1984)*

In the ancient world, a "list of sins" was an itemized bond nailed to a prisoner's cell door — a written record of every crime for which the prisoner had been convicted. When the sentence was served or restitution paid, the authorities removed the list and wrote "paid in full" across it. The prisoner kept that document as proof he could never be tried for those crimes again.

Colossians 2:13-14 says God "forgave all your sins, having canceled the charge of our legal indebtedness, which stood against us and condemned us; he has taken it away, nailing it to the cross." Christ nailed your list to the cross. The believer who trusts that payment can never be tried for those sins again.

**Application for repentance:** When we confess sin (1 John 1:9), we're not re-earning forgiveness — we're agreeing with God about what already happened at the cross. The sin is already paid for. Confession is the act of walking back into that reality rather than trying to pay the debt yourself through guilt or performance.

### CRU: Three-Factor Definition of Confession
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in God's Love," 1984)*

Confession involves at least three factors:
1. Agree with God that you have sinned.
2. Agree that God has already forgiven you (because of the cross).
3. Trust God to change your attitudes and actions — away from the sin, back toward Him.

**Teaching note:** This is theologically tighter than most people's working definition of confession, which stops at factor 1 (acknowledgment). The CRU version includes the turning (3) and, crucially, grounds the confession in what is already true (2) rather than making forgiveness dependent on the quality of our remorse. This maps directly onto the repentance rhythm this lesson is trying to establish.

---

### CRU: Practical Confession Exercise
*(Source: CRU Classic Follow-Up Series, "Your New Life in God's Love," 1984)*

A practical exercise for closing this lesson or assigning as homework:
1. Ask God to reveal sin in your life. Be completely honest — this is between you and God.
2. Write the list on a piece of paper.
3. Write 1 John 1:9 across the list.
4. Thank God for the forgiveness provided through the cross.
5. Tear up the list and throw it away.

**Teaching note:** This exercise gives the lesson a physical, embodied application. The act of writing and then destroying the list gives some people permission to actually let go of guilt they've been carrying. It also reinforces that confession is not just emotional — it is a concrete act of agreement and release.
