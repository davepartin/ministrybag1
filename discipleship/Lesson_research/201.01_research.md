## Lesson Writing Note — Shema Pronunciation
*(Flag for when the lesson is written)*

When this lesson is titled or introduced, include the phonetic pronunciation of the Shema alongside the word. **Shema** is pronounced **"sheh-MAH"** (accent on the second syllable). It is the Hebrew word for "hear" — the opening word of Deuteronomy 6:4: *"Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one."* This is the passage Jesus is quoting when He names the Greatest Commandment. Many people in the room will have never heard the word before; giving them the pronunciation in the title or at first introduction removes the barrier and lets them engage rather than wonder how to say it.

## Load-Bearing Walls
*(The 3–4 ideas this lesson cannot be taught without)*

1. **Everyone is searching for the same thing — and most people can't name it.** The restlessness people feel — chasing satisfaction through praise, power, pleasure, relationships, achievement — is not a character flaw. It's a design feature pointing to a missing piece. Randy Pope names it precisely: *glory*. We were crowned with it (Psalm 8:5), lost it in the Fall, and have been searching for counterfeits ever since. The Greatest Commandment is not another burden. It is the invitation back to what everyone is already looking for.

2. **We love God because He loved first.** The Greatest Commandment (Matthew 22:37) is a response, not an origination. "We love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19) is the engine underneath the command. People don't love God by trying harder — they love God by encountering how much He already loves them. The Echo of Love framework: you can't echo what you haven't received.

**Dave's New Tongue Analogy** *(use to illustrate how a new heart produces new loves)*

> Loving God is like getting a new tongue. When we taste something we don't like, we spit it out — we can't help it. But if you got a tongue transplant, you might find yourself liking things you never could before. God doesn't just change our behavior; He gives us a new heart. What we once ran from — the love of the Father, His presence, His ways — we now want to run toward and be embraced by. New heart, new loves. What you want changes when who you are changes.

*This is the Prodigal Son in a sentence — the son who ran away now runs home. The heart transformation is not willpower; it's a new nature encountering the character of the Father. Place this illustration in the transition between "We love because He first loved us" and the Receive step of the Echo of Love framework.*

3. **The Father's love holds both grace and holiness without collapsing into either.** The lesson must not drift into sentiment. The "Father's love doesn't lower the standard — it changes the heart that meets it" is the guardrail. The older brother in the Prodigal Son parable is the lesson's mirror for everyone doing religion right and still feeling empty — because they have not yet received the Father's love, they have only been managing their performance record.

4. **The Prodigal Son is a story about the Father, not the sons.** Both sons are lost. The younger one is lost in sin; the older one is lost in religion. The Father runs for both. The lesson's anchor is the running Father — the most shocking image of God's pursuing love in all of Scripture.

## The Opening Hook: Randy Pope's Glory Framework
*(Use to open the lesson — before introducing the Greatest Commandment)*

**Source:** Randy Pope, *Life on Life Missional Discipleship* — "Loving God" (BE section)

Pope's central diagnostic: If there is one thing all people — religious or irreligious — have in common, it is that everyone is searching for something that will satisfy them permanently. We describe it as happiness, meaning, significance, hope, fulfillment. But most people never find it. Why?

The answer, Pope argues, is *glory*. Psalm 8:5 says God "crowned [humanity] with glory and honor." That crowning piece was designed to complete us — but the first generation of people forfeited it by trying to establish their own glory, independent of God. Romans 1:21-23 describes the result: humanity "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images made to look like mortal man." The all-important crowning piece went missing, and we have been searching for it in counterfeits ever since — *"an addiction to praise, power, and pleasure as we go from toy to toy, fix to fix, and relationship to relationship."*

The hope: Colossians 1:27 — *"Christ in you, the hope of glory."*

**Why this opens the lesson:** Before you tell people they need to love God, you show them that what they've been searching for all their lives is exactly what God offers. The Greatest Commandment stops being a religious obligation and becomes the answer to the question they've been asking since adolescence.

**Editorial note:** Pope's material on Imago Dei and image-bearing overlaps with 201.02. Keep the glory-as-missing-piece framework here; let the mirror analogy and image-bearing content live in 201.02.

## The Key Framework: Echo of Love

**Receive → Respond → Remain**

- **Receive:** Encounter the Father's initiating love — who He is, that He runs toward you, that His face is delight not disappointment. You cannot echo what you haven't received.
- **Respond:** The Greatest Commandment is the natural response of someone who has encountered that love. Love God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength — not to earn His affection, but because you are already His child.
- **Remain:** Abide in that love (John 15). The disciplines and rhythms of 201.04 flow from this. Remaining is not striving; it is staying near the source.

**Where 1 John 4:19 lives:** It belongs in the Receive step as the theological anchor — "We love because he first loved us." Introduce Matthew 22:37 as the command, then immediately ask: *how is that possible?* Answer: 1 John 4:19. The command becomes possible because the love was given first.

## The Key Story: Prodigal Son (Luke 15)

**The angle:** This is a story about the Father, not the sons. Both sons are lost — one in sin, one in religion. The Father runs for both.

**The running Father:** The father in the parable sees the son "while he was still a long way off" — meaning the father was watching for him. He ran. He kissed him. He threw a party. The robe, the ring, the fatted calf — all extravagant, all before the son has done anything to earn restoration. This is the image of God's pursuing love that anchors the whole lesson.

**The older brother:** Gets a cameo, not a full scene. He is the mirror for discipleship audiences — people who have been doing everything right, keeping the rules, and still feeling resentful and empty. The father goes out to him too. The question the older brother can't answer is the same question the lesson is asking: *have you ever received the Father's love, or have you only been managing your performance record?*

**Why this story:** Every person in the room has heard it. But most have heard it as a story about sin and forgiveness. Retold with the Father as the central character, it becomes a story about the pursuing love of God — which is entirely different and far more powerful.

## Dave's Sermon Archive — Must Use
*(Pulled from NotebookLM search of Dave Partin sermons)*

**The Boss vs. Father Illustration** *(from Dave's Galatians series — primary supporting illustration)*
> *"I think we too often see God as a BOSS. But he is a kind boss. He hired us off the street. But now that we are in the company then we'd better stay in line... But what about a Father who owns a small shop and knows we are not going to be that good at running His company. We struggle in life. But the Father says, I want you to work here. And He says, I know you will fail, but I will be right here with you the whole time."*
> — Dave Partin, Galatians series

Security is based on "the love of the Father and the child's identity in the Family — not the skill set of the child." Use this after the Prodigal Son story to make the contrast concrete. The Boss is watching for mistakes. The Father is watching for you to come home. These are completely different relationships producing completely different hearts.

**"Christianity Is a Religion of ALL"** *(from Dave's Matthew 22 sermon)*
Loving God with all your heart, soul, and mind means everything — but the pivot is critical: "This is how our Creator works. This is how our Savior works. He gave His all for us so that we can have a restored relationship with God." We don't give our ALL to earn His love. We give our ALL in response to His. This is the Echo of Love in Dave's own preaching voice.

**"We Are Not Fighting Sin to Have a Relationship with God"** *(Dave Partin — signature line)*
> *"We are not fighting sin to have a relationship with God. We are fighting sin because we have a relationship with God."*

This line belongs near the end of the lesson as the application turn. It reframes the entire moral life of the Christian — not as effort to earn standing, but as the natural response of someone who already has it. Also threads forward into 101.07 (Repentance), 202.09 (Purity), and 202.08 (Sanctification).

**John 15 Sermon Quote** *(on commandments becoming joy)*
> *"By keeping His commandments we are displaying something internal has happened. The things of God are no longer burdensome but joy."*
> — Dave Partin, John 15 sermon

Use this to support the Key Line: "The Father's love doesn't lower the standard — it changes the heart that meets it." The standard doesn't go down. The heart goes up.

**Zephaniah 3:17 — "He Rejoices Over You With Gladness"**
Most believers don't know this verse — which is exactly why it lands hard. God doesn't just tolerate His children. He rejoices over them. He delights in them. This is the direct answer to the Key Question: *"What changes tomorrow if God's face toward you is delight, not disappointment?"* The answer is already in Scripture. Add this verse to the teaching moment around the Key Question. Pairs with Romans 8:1 (no condemnation) as a one-two punch: no condemnation from God, and beyond that — delight.
