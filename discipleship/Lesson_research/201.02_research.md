## Load-Bearing Walls
*(The 3–4 ideas this lesson cannot be taught without)*

1. **You were made to image.** John Piper captures it best: humans were not just made *as* image-bearers, we were made *to image* — to display, reflect, and communicate who God is. Images are made to image rightly. This is your original design before sin cracked the mirror.

2. **Sin broke the image, Jesus restores it.** The cross is not just forgiveness — it is the restoration of a cracked image. "Reallocated justice" — the injustice done to Jesus was actually justice redirected so that sinful people could be restored to their purpose as image-bearers.

3. **Identity precedes performance.** The Father names you before you do anything. The baptism of Jesus is the clearest proof — "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased" was spoken before a single miracle, a single sermon, a single act of ministry. Beloved before performance. That is the operating system this lesson is installing.

4. **Living from identity instead of striving toward it.** The practical shift is from "I obey, therefore I'm accepted" to "I'm accepted, therefore I obey." Religion runs on the first. The gospel runs on the second. This lesson is the moment the new operating system gets installed.

## The Key Framework: Identity Exchange

What I lost in the Fall → What I gained in the Son.

| Lost in the Fall | Gained in the Son |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Guilty | Justified |
| Orphan | Adopted |
| Dead | Alive |

Simple enough to draw on a whiteboard. Visual enough to photograph and send to a friend. The three rows cover the legal (guilt/justification), relational (orphan/adoption), and spiritual (death/life) dimensions of what Jesus accomplished.

**The Keller move to use inside the lesson:** "Religion says: I obey, therefore I'm accepted. The gospel says: I'm accepted, therefore I obey." This is widely known but still one of the clearest articulations of the identity shift. Use it to introduce the framework, not as the Key Line.

## The Key Story: Jesus' Baptism (Matthew 3)

Before Jesus preached a single sermon, healed a single person, or performed a single miracle — before his public ministry began at all — the Father spoke over him: *"This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased."*

Identity declared before performance. Beloved before useful.

This is the anchor story for the lesson because it doesn't just illustrate the point — it *is* the point, modeled by Jesus himself. The person sitting in your group was baptized in 101. They have their own moment to point to. The callback from 101.02 (their baptism) to this lesson is real: the Father declared something over Jesus at his baptism, and He declared something over you at yours.

## Dave's Writing: The Mirror Analogy
*(Primary supporting illustration — "must use" in lesson body)*

John Piper's framework of being made "to image" — treating image-bearing as an active verb rather than a static noun — is the theological root of this illustration. Building from Piper, the mirror analogy makes it visual and concrete:

> *"Say the glory of God was straight up above us, yet people rarely looked up to see Him. So He designed humans to be mirrors set at a 45-degree angle — so that when people see other people, they actually see God. He is a God of endless power, love, and purpose. He made man to display Himself to the world, for the world to know Him and glorify Him."*
> — Dave Partin, drawing from John Piper's "to image" framework

This is the image people will carry out the door. It's original, it's drawable, and it answers the "so what" of Imago Dei better than almost any other illustration in the lesson. The application turn: you can't reflect God rightly while living as an orphan with a cracked mirror. Adoption through Jesus is what restores the 45-degree angle.

**Where to place it:** After the Identity Exchange framework has landed — once people understand what was restored — the mirror analogy shows them *why it matters to the world around them.* It moves the lesson from personal (what Jesus did for me) to missional (what I now do for others).

## Dave's Writing: The Theological Foundation
*(Use in the opening section of the lesson)*

> *"There is no other event in history more confusing at first look than the death of Jesus. He was a sinless man that was falsely charged by a mob, tortured, and crucified as a criminal. How does this make any sense? But what if there is a greater mission? What if all this injustice was actually reallocated justice? What if God wanted to display His glory, His value, His love, so brightly, so that the whole world could understand how much God wants to pursue sinful people, and how they could know and enjoy Him? So Jesus became the bridge between sinful man and perfect God. Man gets to enjoy a relationship with His Creator, and God is known as the Sacrificial Hero who offers salvation to all who receive it."*
> — Dave Partin

The phrase **"reallocated justice"** is worth developing further. It's fresh, it's precise, and it reframes the cross in a way that answers the confusion people feel when they first encounter it.

## God's Glory and Our Satisfaction
*(Use as the application landing — connects to NC's mission)*

> *"There is a joy in fulfilling one's purpose. God's glory is the 'weight' of all His attributes. And as God's chosen children live out their lives enjoying God and following the ways of Jesus, they become image-bearers, mirrors, pointers to God for others. He has endless love and grace for everyone who repents and believes, and the adopted children can freely go and bring others to their Father. This is why at Neighborhood Church we are about loving God and being His satisfied children."*
> — Dave Partin

This paragraph belongs near the end of the lesson as the application turn — it lands the theology of image-bearing in NC's own mission language and sends people out with a purpose, not just a doctrine.
