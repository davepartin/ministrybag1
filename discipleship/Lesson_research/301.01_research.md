## The Key Framework: The Three Questions

Mapped directly to the lesson's three-word title — Character, Integrity, Accountability.
Leaders can run this self-diagnostic weekly.

1. **Who are you when no one's watching?** *(Character)*
2. **What do you do when you fail?** *(Integrity)*
3. **Who has permission to speak into your life?** *(Accountability)*

---

## Load-Bearing Walls
*(The 3–4 ideas this lesson cannot be taught without)*

1. **Character is the soil, not the fruit.** Leadership skills, communication, and vision are the fruit. Character is the soil everything grows from — and toxic soil produces toxic fruit no matter how talented the gardener.

2. **Paul leads with who you are, not what you can do.** The Greek *anepileptos* ("above reproach") means no legitimate charge could stick — not because you're hiding well, but because your life is genuinely ordered. Paul's list in 1 Timothy 3 is overwhelmingly internal before it's ever functional.

3. **Compromise starts small and quiet.** It doesn't begin with catastrophic failure. It begins with superiority, entitlement, seeking human praise, sacred substitutes (replacing prayer with ministry busyness), and cleaning the outside of the cup. Ananias and Sapphira didn't start with a massive public failure — they wanted to live on both sides of the fence.

4. **Accountability is covenantal, not compliance-based.** True accountability is not a checklist. It's "we yoke ourselves to accountability while we're in our right minds, in case someday sin gets a foothold and blinds us to the truth." *(Dave Partin)*

## The Key Story: Jethro and Moses (Exodus 18)

Moses is the man who spoke with God face to face. He led millions of people out of slavery. And his father-in-law had to tell him: *"What you are doing is not good."*

Even the man with the closest relationship with God in the Old Testament had blind spots. He was burning himself out and burning his people out — and he couldn't see it. Jethro saw it from the outside in one day.

The Jethro story covers both humility (Moses accepting correction without defending himself) and accountability (welcoming a structure he didn't invent). Godly leaders don't hoard authority. They recognize their absolute need for others. If Moses needed it, so does every leader in this room.

**Why this story over the others:** It's less expected for a leadership lesson on character. People have heard the Peter-before-and-after story, the Prodigal Son story. Exodus 18 surprises them, and surprise creates retention.

## Supporting Illustrations
*(Use within the lesson — not the anchor, but strong)*

**The Halloween Costume** *(Dave Partin)*
When teaching on integrity, compare the Pharisees to wearing a Halloween mask: "It is like you spend 98% of your Saturday working on your Halloween costume knowing that you will only wear it for 3 minutes at the party. And when you take it off, people will see you for who you are." God doesn't care about the outer shell. He cares about the heart.

**The Shipwreck / Lifeboat** *(Dave Partin)*
When teaching accountability specifically: Paul on the shipwreck sees the sailors secretly lowering a lifeboat to escape. He steps in and stops them. The challenge: *"Do you have people in your life who will cut away the ropes of the boat you are about to climb in?"* True accountability isn't asking "Did you sin this week?" — it's knowing someone well enough to see when you're drifting before you wreck.

**Who You Are at Home** *(Dave Partin, quoting a youth pastor)*
"If you want to know someone's character and how humble or prideful a person is, ask their spouse and kids. Who you are at home is who you are." Use this to introduce the Key Question.

## Anchor Quotes

**Robert Murray M'Cheyne:**
*"It is not great talents God blesses so much as great likeness to Jesus. A holy minister is an awful weapon in the hand of God."*
Use this early — it reframes the whole lesson. The standard isn't impressive, it's holy.

**John Wooden:**
*"Be more concerned with your character than your reputation, because your character is what you really are, while your reputation is merely what others think you are. The true test of a man's character is what he does when no one is watching."*
Wooden is not a Bible teacher, but this quote is clean, clear, and everyone has heard his name — which lowers defenses before the harder theological content lands.

**John Owen (via Piper):**
*"Be killing sin or it will be killing you."*
Use in the integrity section when discussing the quiet compromise. Short, Puritan, unforgettable.

**Peter Scazzero:**
*"Your shadow is the accumulation of untamed emotions, less-than-pure motives, and thoughts that, while largely unconscious, strongly influence and shape your behaviors. It is the damaged but mostly hidden version of who you are."*
Use this when introducing why character formation requires more than behavior management — the shadow follows you even when you're preaching well.

## How Compromise Starts (Warning Signs)

These are the seeds — long before the public failure:

- **Sacred substitutes:** Replacing prayer and Scripture with ministry busyness. The leader gets so skilled at doing ministry they no longer need God to do the work.
- **The lure of human praise:** When a leader's heart isn't captured by God's glory, it chases the approval of people — which paves the way to every other compromise.
- **Entitlement:** Viewing ministry perks as owed. Stretching the purpose of funds. Demanding unreasonable sacrifice from others while protecting personal comfort.
- **Cleaning the outside of the cup:** Maintaining appearances while harboring secret patterns. Pharisee behavior — public discipline, private disorder.
- **Vanity and insecurity together:** Obsessing over image, comparing to other leaders, refusing to delegate because it means losing control.

## Accountability in Practice
*(Practical mechanics — the area the Piper sources are thin on)*

True accountability has layers:
1. **Relational prudence** — Specific boundaries (opposite-sex meetings, etc.) that protect the leader's marriage and reputation
2. **Financial integrity** — External checks; the leader gives generously to their own church, and someone knows it
3. **Board oversight** — A healthy leader invites honest dialogue about blind spots, not just approval of decisions
4. **Vulnerability with at least one internal + one external partner** — Someone who knows the "good, the bad, and the ugly" of the inner life

The goal is Hebrews 10:24-25 accountability: *"Consider how to stir up one another to love and good works."* Not compliance enforcement — gospel application. Partners who sit with you in failure and preach grace to you so you can rise and fight again. *(Piper: "gutsy guilt")*

**Gap to fill:** Piper is theologically rich but thin on the logistics — how often to meet, specific questions, structural firewalls for church planters. Pull from church planting practical resources for this section.

## Theological Distinctive to Protect

We do not fight for character to *earn* a relationship with God. We fight for purity and integrity *because* we already have one. Character is not born from a debtor's ethic — it is the overflow of joy and satisfaction in God. We pursue integrity from freedom, leaning on future grace. *(Piper)*

This matters most in how the lesson lands. If it ends as a lecture about trying harder, it has failed. If it ends with leaders understanding their identity is already secure and they're fighting from that security, it will produce the fruit it's calling for.
